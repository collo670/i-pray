Second Reading

Book 5: 17, 3 – 4
The economy of the cross has revealed to us the hidden Word.

A reading from the treatise “Against the heresies” of St. Irenaeus, bishop.
“Blessed is the man to whom the Lord imputes no evil” (Ps. 31, 21) showing in precedence that remission that is realised with His coming, remission thanks to which is annulled “the written document of our debt, nailing it to the cross” (Col. 2, 14); in a way that, as through a piece of wood we had become debtors of God, so also by means of a piece of wood we have received the remission of our debt. This is shown in a significant way also by means of many others, as already also through the prophet Elisha. While in fact the prophets who were with him cut the wood to construct the tabernacle, the blade of the axe detached itself and fell into the Jordan, and they were unable to find it again, Elisha having arrived and having heard of what had happened, threw a piece of wood into the water; as soon as he had done this, the iron of the axe floated up to the surface and from there those who had lost it were able to recover it. In this way the prophet showed that the solid Word of God, lost by us through negligence because of a piece of wood, and which we were not capable of finding anymore, we would newly receive by means of a piece of wood. The Word of God is truly similar to an axe, and of it John the Baptist says: “Already the axe has been placed to the roots of the trees” (Mt. 3, 10). Similarly Jeremiah says: The word of God is “ like a hammer that breaks the rock” (Jer. 23, 29). Therefore as we have said, it was actually a piece of wood that revealed to us again the Word that was hidden to us. As in fact we had lost him because of a piece of wood, so also through a piece of wood He was revealed to all, showing in himself the height, the length and the breadth; as one of the apostles says, he reconciled to only one God the two peoples by stretching His arms (cfr. Ep. 2, 16); two were the hands, and two were the people who were dispersed up to the extreme boundaries of the earth; But one only was the head that reunited them, because “ there is only one God, who is above all, who acts through all and is present in all” (Ep. 4, 6).

Second Reading

Ps. 43, 83 – 85
The sign of Jonah.

A reading from the “Commentary on the Psalms” of St. Ambrose, bishop.
Let us examine if what we read in Jonah is far away from the Gospel; that is that he was sleeping profoundly in the bottom of the boat: a prefiguration of the holy passion. As in fact Jonah was sleeping in the boat and snoring tranquilly, without fear of being captured, so also Our Lord Jesus Christ, who fulfils that pre-figuration with the mystery of His death, at the time of the preaching of the Gospel slept in the boat; and as Jonah had “remained three days and three nights in the belly of the fish, so also the Son of man remained three days and three nights in the heart of the earth” (Mt, 12, 40) during His passion. When He awoke from death, reawakening His body from sleep, He arose for the universal salvation and visited his disciples. He, therefore is the true Jonah, who gives His life to redeem us. He had been taken and thrown into the sea because, captured and devoured by the whale, He could purify her living inside her. Of which whale are we referring to, listen to Job: “Am I perhaps a sea monster, so that you have to capture me?” (Jb. 7, 12) Who is this one? You know Him when you read that our Lord Jesus Christ “ascending into heaven took with Him prisoners” (Ep. 4, 8). The enemy in fact having been defeated, we who were slaves have started to possess through Christ, freedom. Besides this the same plea of the holy Jonah teaches us that it is dealing with the mysteries of the passion of the Lord; in fact he says “In my anguish I invoked the Lord and He listened to me; from the depths of hell I cried” (Joh. 2, 3). Do you see that he speaks of the depths of hell, not of the whale? The Lord in fact descended not into the whale, but into hell in order to liberate from prison also those who were found there. Who is it who fulfils the sacrifice praising and confessing the Lord, if not the first of all the priests, who prayed and implored for everyone? He alone could obtain a similar result. When Jonah was thrown into the sea, the sea became calm; so also our Lord Jesus Christ came into this world in order to save it, reconciling every thing with His blood, both in heaven and on earth (Cfr. Col. 1, 20). With His coming He redeemed all of humanity and with His works, raising the dead, curing the sick, infusing into the hearts of men the fear of God, He brought them to adore Him. He is the one who offers for us to God a sacrifice of salvation, offering a victim that is worthy for our conversion; He is the one who after having slept has awakened forever.

Third Reading

Disc. 76: 1, 4, 5, 6, 8, 9
Lord, I am lost: save me!

A reading from the “Discourses” of St. Augustine, bishop.
The gospel that has been read speaks of Christ the Lord who walked on the water of the sea and of the apostle Peter who while walking on the water hesitated because of fear; and mistrusting sank, but when he had found trust again he re-emerged. This reminds us that the sea is the world and Peter is the symbol of the only Church. The same Peter in fact, first in the order of the apostles, very generous in love towards Christ, often answered, he alone, in the name of everyone. When the Lord Jesus Christ asked them who men said he was, while the disciples put forward the various opinions of the people, to the repeated question of the Lord “And you, who do you say I am?” (Mk. 8: 29), it was Peter who answered: “You are the Christ, the Son of the living God” (Mt. 16: 16). He alone answers in the name of the others, expressing in this way the unity. Contemplating this member of the Church, we distinguish in him that which is of God from that which is ours. In this way we learn not to be doubtful, but we base ourselves on the rock, unmoveable and constant against the winds, rain and rivers, that is against the temptations of this world. Observe therefore Peter, who then was the figure of all of us: now he trusts, now doubts, now he considers himself immortal, now he is afraid of dying. Since the Church of Christ is formed by the strong and by the weak, it is inevitable that there is one and the other. That which Peter proclaimed: “You are the Christ of God”, shows the behaviour of the strong; when instead he is afraid and hesitates, fears death not recognising the life, and desiring that the Christ should not suffer, he symbolises the weak who form a part of the Church. This is the meaning of what has been read until now: “Lord, if it is you, command that I come to you over the water” (Mt 14: 28). Peter walked over the water at the command of the Lord, knowing well that he would not have been able to do so with his own strength. Through faith he fulfilled that which human weakness could not do. These are the strong ones of the Church. The Man-God commanded and the man was able to fulfil the impossible. “Come!” (Mt. 14: 29) he said and “getting out of the boat, he began to walk over the water”. Peter could do it, because he who is the Rock had commanded it. This is what Peter became in the Lord; and what is he in himself? “But because of the violence of the wind he became afraid and, beginning to sink, he cried out: Lord, save me!” (Mt. 14: 30). When he trusted in the Lord, he had strength from him. When as a man he became anxious, he turned again to the Lord, and he, offering immediately the help of his right hand, caught him while he was sinking, rebuking him for his diffidence: “Man of little faith!” (Mt. 14: 31). Let us conclude, brothers. Consider that the world is like the sea: violent wind, furious tempest. For each one his passions are a tempest. If you love God, you walk on the water and the fury of the world is under your feet. If you love the world, it will overwhelm you: it in fact knows how to devour those who love it, not to sustain them. But when your heart is agitated by the passions, invoke the divinity of Christ in order to overcome them. And if your foot wavers, if you doubt, if you are not able to overcome something, if you begin to sink, exclaim: “Lord, save me!” (Mt. 14: 30). Only he in fact frees you from the death of the flesh, he who in his flesh has died for you.

Third Reading

Book. 6: 51-52
The bread that I will give is my flesh for the life of the world.

A reading from the “Commentary on the Gospel of John” of Rupert of Deutz, abbot.
“I am the bread that has come down from heaven. If anyone eats of this bread he will live forever” (Jn. 6: 51). Since the guests of my Father were destroyed by death because of the prohibited food that their parents had eaten, and their souls are in hell, the bodies instead in the tombs, I also who am the bread of angels will be destroyed. And with that substance of which the angels are nourished I will descend into the underworld, where the souls are hungry, while with the body I will rest hidden for three days and three nights in the same womb of the earth in which their bodies are buried, as Jonah remained three days and three nights in the belly of the sea mammal. In this way their souls will be recreated in the sight of God and many bodies will rise now; all the others certainly in the future. Finally, to those who are still in the body down here, this same bread will be offered in a way that is suitable to the living, that is in the true sacrifice of the bread and wine according to the order of Melchizedek. “And the bread that I will give is my flesh for the life of the world” (Jn. 6: 51). This is the greatest consolation for the poor, that the Holy Spirit descending on me sends me to evangelise; this will be the greatest and incomparable motive of joy for all the nations spread over the earth, who will ask and will receive from the Father in possession and inheritance. And those to whom the Father has sent me will not have less than the ancient fathers of this bread of life that he has given and on whom “he has put his seal” (Jn. 6: 44, 27). In fact, when I will have descended to them to fill them with myself, after hell had stung me and I had become its sting and the death of death which it enclosed in its bowels, and I had reached the starving saints and just men in order to give life back to them: then I will give to those who have remained that bread, in which the reality of my own flesh is present, that is that body which, having come out of the belly of the sea mammal, healthy and complete will reside eternally at the right hand of the Father. The man who is still living will feed in a way that is to him in keeping with the same bread of angels which I will give to him, and which the Father gives to those who are not anymore of this earth so that they may eat it and rise, now with the soul, and on the last day also with the body. “And the bread that I will give is my flesh for the life of the world” (Jn. 6: 51), and truly the Father gives the bread of angels so that he could take a body and he could die in order to give life to the dead. This same heavenly bread gives us an earthly bread that transforms into his flesh, in order to give eternal life to the living who can eat it. And so the Word, who is the bread of angels, became flesh, not changing himself into flesh, but assuming a body; so also the same Word already made flesh becomes visible bread, not because he is changed into bread but because he assumes and transfers the bread into the unity of his person. As a consequence, as through the unity of the person we recognise the true God in our flesh generated by the Virgin Mary, so also we confess with full and catholic faith that this visible bread, assumed and transformed by the invisible divinity of the Word in his flesh, is truly the Body of Christ. In fact he says: “The bread that I will give is my flesh for the life of the world” (Jn. 6: 51), that is because the redeemed world eats and lives, after having washed in baptism that original sin committed with the ancient food that the serpent gave and induced to eat.

Third Reading

Disc. 15: 11-13
The word of God is the light for my steps.

A reading from the “Commentary on Psalm 118” of St. Ambrose, bishop
The faith will accompany you on the way, the divine Scripture will be your road. The Word of God is a good guide. Light up your light to this lamp, so that your interior eye, which is the lamp of your body, will be illuminated. You have many lamps: light them all up because it has been said to you: “See that you are dressed for action and have your lamps lit” (Lk. 12: 35). Where the darkness is great, many lamps are necessary and in much darkness the light of our merits must shine forth. These are the lamps that the law has established to shine continuously in the tabernacle of the testimony. It represents our body, in which Christ came, who “through a greater and more perfect tent, entered once and for all into the sanctuary with his own blood” (Heb. 9: 11, 12) in order to purify our soul from all works of death and from every stain. Now in our bodies, which with the quality of their actions manifest our hidden thoughts, must always shine the clear light of our virtues like as many lamps. These are the burning lamps that shine day and night in the temple of God. If you keep your body as the temple of God, if your members are the members of Christ, your virtues shine with the clear light and no one, other than your sin, can put it off. Of this light of pure minds and sincere affections shines our solemnities. Therefore let your lamp always shine. The Christ rebukes also those who, though possessing the lamp, do not keep it always lit, saying: “See that you are dressed for action and have your lamps lit” (Lk. 12: 35). We must not enjoy only for a short time of this light. He enjoys only for a short time the one who, after having listened to the word in the Church and having rejoiced, as soon as he goes out forgets everything and does not care. This one walks in his house without light, since he is in the darkness and does works of darkness, clothed with the clothes of the devil, not of Christ. This is what happens when the lamp of the world is not lit. Let us never neglect the word of the Lord, which is for us the source of every virtue and progress of all our works. If the members of the body cannot work well without the light- the feet in fact are uncertain and the hands make mistakes-, how much more must the steps of the soul and the operations of our mind be illuminated by the light of the Word. As Thomas touched the signs of the resurrection of the Lord, so also in the light of the word the hands of the soul do not err. This lamp remains lit in every word and in every work. All our interior and external steps are directed towards it.
