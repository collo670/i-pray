Second Reading

Nm. 12 – 16, 19
All these things happened to them as a figure.

A reading from “the Treatise on the mysteries” of St. Ambrose, bishop.
There is also a third testimony, as the Apostle teaches us: “For all our fathers were under the cloud, and all passed through the sea, and were all baptised in relation to Moses in the cloud and in the sea.” And further, Moses himself says in his song: “You sent Your Spirit, and the sea covered them.” You observe that even then holy baptism was prefigured in that passage of the Hebrews, wherein the Egyptian perished, the Hebrew escaped. For what else are we daily taught in this sacrament but that guilt is swallowed up and error done away, but that virtue and innocence remain unharmed? You hear that our fathers were under the cloud, and that a kindly cloud, which cooled the heat of carnal passions. That kindly cloud overshadows those whom the Holy Spirit visits. At last it came upon the Virgin Mary, and the Power of the Highest overshadowed her, when she conceived Redemption for the race of men. And that miracle was wrought in a figure through Moses. If, then, the Spirit was in the figure, is He not present in the reality, since Scripture says to us: “For the law was given by Moses, but grace and truth came by Jesus Christ.” Marah was a fountain of most bitter water: Moses cast wood into it and it became sweet. For water without the preaching of the Cross of the Lord is of no avail for future salvation, but, after it has been consecrated by the mystery of the saving cross, it is made suitable for the use of the spiritual laver and of the cup of salvation. As, then, Moses, that is, the prophet, cast wood into that fountain, so, too, the priest utters over this font the proclamation of the Lord’s cross, and the water is made sweet for the purpose of grace. You must not trust, then, wholly to your bodily eyes; that which is not seen is more really seen, for the object of sight is temporal, but that other eternal, which is not apprehended by the eye, but is discerned by the mind and spirit. Lastly, let the lessons lately gone through from the Kings teach you. Naaman was a Syrian, and suffered from leprosy, nor could he be cleansed by any. Then a maiden from among the captives said that there was a prophet in Israel, who could cleanse him from the defilement of the leprosy. And it is said that, having taken silver and gold, he went to the king of Israel. And he, when he heard the cause of his coming, rent his clothes, saying, that occasion was rather being sought against him, since things were asked of him which pertained not to the power of kings. Elisha, however, sent word to the king, that he should send the Syrian to him, that he might know there was a God in Israel. And when he had come, he bade him dip himself seven times in the river Jordan. Then he began to reason with himself that he had better waters in his own country, in which he had often bathed and never been cleansed of his leprosy; and so remembering this, he did not obey the command of the prophet, yet on the advice and persuasion of his servants he yielded and dipped himself. And being forthwith cleansed, he understood that it is not of the waters but of grace that a man is cleansed. And he indeed doubted before he was healed; you are already healed, and therefore ought not to doubt.

Obadiah 1 – 21
Prediction against Edom.

A reading from the prophet Obadiah.

Behold, I will make you small among the nations, you shall be utterly despised. The pride of your heart has deceived you, you who live in the clefts of the rock, whose dwelling is high, who say in your heart, "Who will bring me down to the ground?" Though you soar aloft like the eagle, though your nest is set among the stars, thence I will bring you down, says the LORD. On the day that you stood aloof, on the day that strangers carried off his wealth, and foreigners entered his gates and cast lots for Jerusalem, you were like one of them. But you should not have gloated over the day of your brother in the day of his misfortune; you should not have rejoiced over the people of Judah in the day of their ruin; you should not have boasted in the day of distress. You should not have entered the gate of my people in the day of his calamity; you should not have gloated over his disaster in the day of his calamity; you should not have looted his goods in the day of his calamity. The house of Jacob shall be a fire, and the house of Joseph a flame, and the house of Esau stubble; they shall burn them and consume them, and there shall be no survivor to the house of Esau; for the LORD has spoken. Second Reading

You who are sick, come and get yourself cured.

Beginning of the “Discourse on baptism” of St. Gregory of Nyssa, bishop.
Behold: the dispenser of every good, he who has estimated the years and holds the dominion of time, he has brought us to the day of salvation, in which it is normal for us to render the guests into adoptive sons, the poor participants of grace, those who were polluted, purified of the sins. This is the old proclamation that was present a little before the Lord could appear: “Voice of one who cries in the desert: Prepare the way of the Lord, make his paths straight” (Mt. 3:3; cf: Is. 40: 3). I am neither John nor David, but my smallness does not bear any prejudice to the law of the Lord. We do not obey because of reverence towards those who have written the laws, but we submit with fear to the power and the orders of He who makes the laws. The grace of the King has arrived, which has conceded remission to two classes of tortured people: he has freed the prisoners and has remitted the debts of the debtors. For this, I also can offer a suitable medicine to these two categories of people, and with great faith I promise that, if they commit themselves, they will receive help. And so that no one thinks that this medicine costs a lot, I announce clearly what medication the sick have to use: to the first I promise health through the waters of baptism, to the others I destroy the evil by means of a few tears. This simple medication and the grace of God is enough for such a great result: to be freed from the wounds inflicted on us by the bite of the serpent, without having to burn and to cut in order to be healed. Come therefore, you who suffer, to get yourselves cured and may you not be neglected. When a sickness is continuous and old, the medical skill and care can do nothing. Poor and needy, hurry: come and receive the gifts of the King; and you, sheep, welcome the character and the sign of the cross, help and remedy against evils. Give me your names: I will write them with ink on corruptible books, but God will engrave them on tablets that do not perish, because He writes with His own finger as He wrote once the law for the Hebrews. Come to him and you will be resplendent and your faces will not be confused (Cfr. Ps. 33:6). Wash yourselves, remove from my sight the evil of your actions” (Is. 1:16). These words were written a long time ago, but their importance has not weakened, on the contrary day by day it is in full vigour. Come out from prison, I implore you. Hate the dark places of vice. Flee from the devil, cruel guardian of the one who is in chains, he who feeds of the misfortunes of the sinners and those who go in search of him. As God rejoices in our just works, so also the author of sin is delighted for the crimes. Abandon the old man like dirty clothes, full of filth and undignified because it is stained by thousands of sins and made with the cloth of iniquity. Receive the vestment of incorruptibility, that Christ Himself offers you already ready to be put on, and do not refuse the gift in order not to offend the one who offers it to you. You have rolled in the mud a long time: hurry to the Jordan: John calls you, but it is Christ who entreats you. The river of grace runs everywhere: it does not have its sources in Palestine nor does it empty itself in the sea nearby, but it irrigates the whole world, it enters into paradise, and making an inverted course to the four rivers that flow out of it, it brings you much more precious things than those that have come out of it. Its rich source is Christ: flowing from Him, it floods the whole world. It has sweet and drinkable waters, without irritating salt. It is rendered sweet by the presence of the Spirit, like the fountain of Marah because of its contact with wood.

Third Reading

P. II. C.3
Christ in his majesty is always desirable.

A reading from the work “The sacrament of the Altar” of Baldwin of Canterbury, bishop
“They who eat me will hunger for more, they who drink me will thirst for more” (Sir. 24: 20). In the present time we cannot nourish ourselves with Christ, wisdom of God, up to the point of satisfying our desire, but remaining with the desire of satisfying ourselves; and all the more we taste his sweetness, the more our desire is heightened. Therefore those who eat will hunger for more, until they reach satiety. When the desire of the good will be satisfied they will not have either hunger or thirst anymore. What has been said can also be applied to the future life: “They who eat me will hunger for more, they who drink me will thirst for more”. There is in fact in that eternal satiety, as a longing, coming not from destitution but from beatitudes: in heaven those who do not need anymore to eat desire always to eat, they are never nauseated by their satiety. There is in fact satiety without nuisance and a desire without agony. Christ, who is always marvellous in his majesty, constitutes unceasingly the object of our desire, he in whom “even the angels long to catch a glimpse” (1 Pet. 1: 12). This is why though possessing him, we desire him and also when we have reached him we continue to search for him, as it is written: “Search for his face always” (Ps. 104: 4). We search continuously for he whom we love in order to be possessed by him eternally. Therefore those who find him continue to search for him, those who eat of him are still hungry, and those who drink of him are still thirsty; but this hunger takes away all other hungers and this thirst extinguishes all other thirsts. It does not come from privation, but from happiness that is accomplished. Of that hunger which is caused by privation it is said in fact: “He who comes to me will never be hungry again and he who believes in me will not be thirsty again” (Jn. 6: 35). Of the hunger that comes from the beatitudes instead it is said: “They who eat me will hunger for more, they who drink of me will thirst for more”. For those who believe in him, Christ is food and drink, bread and wine: he is bread and wine because he strengthens and consolidates, drink and wine because he makes happy. All that in us which is strong, valid and constant, the cheerful happiness with which we observe the commandments of God, bear the suffering, obey and fight for justice: so much strength and so much courage comes to us from that bread, the joy from that drink. Blessed are those who act with strength and joy. And since no one can do this with his own strength, blessed are those who desire ardently for that which is just and honest, being in everything comforted and cheered by he who says: “Blessed are those who hunger and thirst for justice” (Mt. 5: 6). Then if Christ is the bread and drink that strengthens and gladdens the just in the present life, the greater will he be in the future the fount of their eternal beatitude!

Third Reading

Disc. 18: 26-29
Christ drinks my bitterness in order to give me the sweetness of his grace.

A reading from the “Commentary on Psalm 118” of St. Ambrose, bishop.
“I am small and despised, but I do not neglect your precepts” (Ps. 118: 141). I have the stately participation in the heavenly goods. Already I am welcomed to the honour of the heavenly table: for my banquets there is no need of rainwater, or of the products of the earth or the fruits of the trees. In order to take away the thirst there is no need to search for rivers or springs: Christ is my food, Christ is my drink, the flesh of a God sustains me, the blood of a God quenches my thirst. In order to satisfy myself I certainly do not have to wait for the annual harvest, since Christ offers himself everyday to me. I will not have a reason to fear that some tempest from heaven or the sterility of the fields will compromise him, provided I persist in a loving and pious devotion. I do not desire that a rain of quails descends on me, that before I admired, and neither the manna, that before I preferred to all the other types of food; because the fathers ate it, and still were hungry. Mine is such a food that if someone eats of it they will not be hungry again; it is a food that does not fatten the body, but strengthens the heart of man. Before I admired the bread from heaven; in fact it is written: “he gave them bread from heaven to eat” (Jn. 6: 31); but it was not that. The true bread, was only a figure of that future one: the Father has put aside the bread from heaven for me, that true one. That bread of God that gives life to the world has descended from heaven for me. This is the bread of life: therefore, he who eats life cannot die. In fact, how can he who nourishes himself of life die? How can he who possesses in himself a vital substance fail? Come to him and satisfy yourself, because he is bread; draw near to him and drink, because he is the source; draw near to him and you will be radiant (cfr. Ps. 33: 6) because he is light; come to him and you will be freed, because “there where the Spirit of the Lord is, there is freedom” (2 Cor. 3: 17); draw near to him and you will be absolved, because he is the remission of sins. Ask who he is? Listen to what he says: “I am the bread of life: he who comes to me will not hunger anymore and he who believes in me will not be thirsty again” (Jn. 6: 35). You have heard, you have seen, and you have not believed in him: for this you are dead; but now believe, in order to be able to live. From the body of God gushes out for me an eternal spring: Christ drinks my bitterness in order to give me the sweetness of his grace.

Third Reading

Disc. 14: 20-22
Let us follow the Word, let us search for that rest.

A reading from the “Discourses” of St. Gregory Nazianzen, bishop.
“Let the sage boast no more of his wisdom, nor the valiant of his valour, nor the rich man of his riches” (Jer. 9: 23), even if he has reached the highest level of wisdom, of riches or of power. I then will add similar things to this: let the one who is famous or renowned not be proud of his glory; nor he who enjoys excellent health for his health; nor he who is beautiful for his attractiveness; nor he who is young for his young age; in a word, let there not be any proud or conceited man who glories in those things that are praised in this world; instead let the one who wants to boast, boast in only one thing: to know and to search for God; and sympathising in the fate of the unfortunate, let him put aside a little good for the future life. All the other things are transient and fragile, and, as in a game of stones, the stones are thrown and transferred from one to the other; so nothing belongs to the one who possesses it, which will not be consumed with the passing of time or transferred to others with displeasure. Those instead are sure and stable realities, which will not lack, or dissolve; the hope of him who puts his own faith in it, will not be frustrated. It seems to me moreover that exactly because no good on this earth is stable and lasting, and whatever other thing made wisely by the creating Word and by that Wisdom which surpasses every mind leaves us deluded, and we see the things changing now in one sense now in another, now transported on high now low or even overturned, and before we can take them in our hands they have already gone far away and escaped from us: exactly for this, I say, seeing their instability and variability, we are pushed and direct ourselves towards the port of the future life. What would we have done if our well-being was secure, while, although fluctuating and fragile, we are attached as though by chains and we are reduced by its deceptive greed and great slavery, that we are not able to think that there is anything better and more precious than the present things? And all this while we are listening and are convinced that we have been created in the image of that God who is in heaven and who attracts us to himself, and of this we are convinced? “Let he who is wise observe these things and he will understand” (Ps. 106: 43). Who will disregard the transient things? Who will await the things that do not change? Who will consider the present things as if they were not existing? Truly blessed is he who, separating with the Word that which is better from the wicked, putting aside and dividing “decides in his heart the holy journey” (Ps. 83: 6), as the blessed David says. And fleeing with all his strength from this valley of tears, he searches for the supreme goods; crucified with Christ to the world, with Christ he rises and together with him he ascends, heir of a new life that is not transitory or false, where the serpent does not bite anymore during the journey, nor lays snares for the heel, having had its head crushed. The same blessed Michael, observing this fact and mocking the reptiles and those who have only the appearance of good, exclaimed: “Come, let us go up to the mountain of the Lord. Get up! Be off! There is no resting here. (Mic. 4: 2; 2: 10). These are almost the same words with which our Lord and Saviour exhorts us: “Get up, let us go away from here” (Jn. 14: 31). Saying this, he moved not only his disciples of that time from that place, as someone could perhaps think, but he detached his disciples forever from the earth and from the earthly goods in order to take them towards heaven and the heavenly things. Let us therefore follow the Word, let us search for that rest; let us despise the riches and the comforts of this life, enriching ourselves only with that which in it is good; that is let us save our souls with almsgiving, distributing our riches to the poor in order to enrich ourselves with the heavenly goods.
