Second Reading

St. Augustine, Serm. 227, 1-2
Christ is the bread of life.

A reading from the "Sermons" of St. Augustine, bishop.
“They who eat me will hunger for more, they who drink me will thirst for more” (Sir. 24: 20). In the present time we cannot nourish ourselves with Christ, wisdom of God, up to the point of satisfying our desire, but remaining with the desire of satisfying ourselves; and all the more we taste his sweetness, the more our desire is heightened. Therefore those who eat will hunger for more, until they reach satiety. When the desire of the good will be satisfied they will not have either hunger or thirst anymore. What has been said can also be applied to the future life: “They who eat me will hunger for more, they who drink me will thirst for more”. There is in fact in that eternal satiety, as a longing, coming not from destitution but from beatitudes: in heaven those who do not need anymore to eat desire always to eat, they are never nauseated by their satiety. There is in fact satiety without nuisance and a desire without agony. Christ, who is always marvellous in his majesty, constitutes unceasingly the object of our desire, he in whom “even the angels long to catch a glimpse” (1 Pet. 1: 12). This is why though possessing him, we desire him and also when we have reached him we continue to search for him, as it is written: “Search for his face always” (Ps. 104: 4). We search continuously for he whom we love in order to be possessed by him eternally. Therefore those who find him continue to search for him, those who eat of him are still hungry, and those who drink of him are still thirsty; but this hunger takes away all other hungers and this thirst extinguishes all other thirsts. It does not torment, it delights.

Third Reading

St. Cyril of Alexandria, In Ioannem, 4, 2
Christ, the living bread.

A reading from the "Commentary on the Gospel of John" of St. Cyril of Alexandria.
“I am the living bread which came down from heaven” (Jn. 6: 51). Since the guests of my Father were destroyed by death because of the prohibited food that their parents had eaten, and their souls are in hell, the bodies instead in the tombs, I also who am the bread of angels will be destroyed. And with that substance of which the angels are nourished I will descend into the underworld, where the souls are hungry, while with the body I will rest hidden for three days and three nights in the same womb of the earth in which their bodies are buried, as Jonah remained three days and three nights in the belly of the sea mammal. In this way their souls will be recreated in the sight of God and many bodies will rise now; all the others certainly in the future. Finally, to those who are still in the body down here, this same bread will be offered in a way that is suitable to the living, that is in the true sacrifice of the bread and wine according to the order of Melchizedek. “And the bread that I will give is my flesh for the life of the world” (Jn. 6: 51). This is the greatest consolation for the poor, that the Holy Spirit descending on me sends me to evangelise; this will be the greatest and incomparable motive of joy for all the nations spread over the earth, who will ask and will receive from the Father in possession and inheritance.

First Reading

Obad. 1-21
The pride of Edom will be brought low.

A reading from the book of the prophet Obadiah.
The vision of Obadiah. Thus says the Lord GOD concerning Edom: We have heard a report from the LORD, and a messenger has been sent among the nations: "Rise up! Let us rise against her for battle!" Behold, I will make you small among the nations; you shall be utterly despised. The pride of your heart has deceived you, you who live in the clefts of the rock, whose habitation is high; who say in your heart, "Who will bring me down to the ground?" Though you soar aloft like the eagle, though your nest is set among the stars, thence I will bring you down, says the LORD.
